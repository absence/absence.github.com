1 Back to the future - p107

Key words: Entropy, Rudolf Clausius, Second law of thermodynamics, Time reversal [AKA T-symmetry] Aristotle, Final cause,

Rudolf Clausius/1865: Entropy - the tendency in nature for things to fall apart.

Second law of thermodynamics: all other things being equal: entropy will go for broke.

Thus: Life and thinking - order and organization - are quite unnatural.

Loophole: Second law is a tendency - but not a necessity. There's room to wiggle here.

In life: Time reversal appears implicit as is Aristotle's 'final cause'.

2 The Law of Effect - p109

Key words: Functions, Daniel Dennett, Darwin's Dangerous Idea, Charles Darwin, The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex Natural selection, Jean-Baptiste Lamarck, Edward Thorndike, Law of effect, Empedocles, Aristotle, Entelechy, Thomas Malthus, Jacques Monod, Chance and Necessity

We know of functions as part of the process of design. Darwin taught us that functions may emerge spontaneously.

Daniel Dennet: logic of natural selection defeats claims that teleogy is necessary to explain complexity of biology

Theory of natural selection = statistics

Darwin: organisms - 1. reproduce, 2, diverge, 3. produce surfeit. Process generating inequalities. Differs with Lamarck's acquired characteristics.

Darwin's discovery/Descent of Man: logic of natural selection is after-the-fact. Favorable outcomes preferentially reproduced, retained or expressed. How these are generated matters not.

Edward Thorndike's Law of effect: organisms adapt their behavior to their environment.

Why did it take so long to discover? Humans are designers. Easier to think there's a supreme designer than to intuit the spontaneity.

Empedocles: orderliness may arrive spontaneously - by preserving accidents. Only combinations that produce balance/fitness likely to persist.

Aristotle disses Empedocles. How could the order and beauty we see be merely accidental? There's an entelechy lurking.

Darwin's thoughts: provoked by Malthus, supervene Larmarck. Mechanisms for reproducing and preserving are entirely separable.

Jacques Monod's Chance and Necessity: systems of molecular biology may be explained without final causality.
