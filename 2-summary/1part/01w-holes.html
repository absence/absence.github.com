1 A stone's throw - p 18

This section:

  • provides descriptions of various aspects of the separation of thought processes from physical processes
  • considers a vision (that really exists from a scientific point of view) of a future based on the past that informs the present

Key words: causality, organization

Topics covered: philosophy, biology.

Parable: a boy skips a stone across water. How can we explain what has happened in a manner that takes into account the myriad thoughts and physical actions that just occured? p18-19

There is a huge discrepancy between what is spontaneouusly probable in the physical [inorganic] realm and the deviant [wildly improbable] introduced by organisms and minds. p20

Even with fully detailed knowledge about the boy's current physical state might only be able to predict the next few seconds of his activity. p20

On the other hand, a glimpse of the boy a year earlier being shown how to skip a stone, could be vastly more predictive. p20

A shift from physical to mental causation produces far better predictive results. 'Ends determining means'. p21

Thoughts are about possibilities. Possibilities are future events - which do not exist and may never exist. And yet possibilities influence the present. p21

This is a reversal of traditional causal knowledge: order developing from disorder. How can we account for this?

4 Telos ex machina - p34

This section:

  • Characterizes the history of thinking on causation
  • Describes such thinking as building metaphors of black boxes within black boxes, machines within machines
  • This type of thinking begs for a watchmaker
  • Note, however, that machines and organisms are quite different
  • Lists differences between machines and organisms

Key words: Aristotle, final causality, Francis Bacon, René Descartes, Baruch Spinoza, William Paley

Topics covered: philosophy, biology

A study of what causes things is ancient. p34

In the realm of Aristotle's four causes: ententional processes are expressions of final causality. p35

Bacon, Descartes, Spinoza: tell us that descibing purpose and value is like discussing black boxes within black boxes. a vicious regression. p34

Science has been highly successful in gradually replacing these black boxes with mechanistic processes. p35

Greatest success in this process: natural selection. p35

Darwin trumps Paley's 'a watch must have a watchmaker'. p35

Gives us metaphor of an immemse machine full of smaller machines. p36

Dawkins characterizes evolution as the blind watchmaker. p36

Note that there are significant differences between machines and organisms [life]. p36

These differences [will be an ongoing topic of discussion] and may be charaterized as follows - p36-37

Machines:

  • are assembled to do something for some end by some external process
  • are engineered artifacts
  • obey an order that derives from outside
  • are built or made
  • arise from bringing together and systematically assembling selected parts
  • have physical behaviors are anticipated
  • are simplifications of the causal world
  • are finite
  • have features and future states are fully describable
  • are closed from variation except as imposed extrinsically
  • are predicted on a conception of causality that excludes and yet assumes teleology
  • beg for a watchmaker

Organisms:

  • are as precise and as integrated as any machine, but little else compares
  • grow by multiplication of cells
  • a process of division and differentiation
  • arise from less differentiated pre-cursors
  • wholes precede parts
  • integration is intrinsic
  • design occurs spontaneously
  • machine metaphor is over simplification, misleading - p36

If we think of ourselves as machines then we also need a watchmaker [deity]. p37
