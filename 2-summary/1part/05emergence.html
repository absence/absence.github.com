Incomplete Nature glossary definition: Emergence: A term used to designate an apparently discontinuous transition from one mode of causal properties to another of a higher rank, typically associated with an increase in scale in which lower-order component interactions contribute global properties that appear irreducible to the lower-order interactions. The term has a long and diverse history, but throughout this history it has been used to describe the way that living and mental processes depend upon chemical and physical processes, yet exhibit collective properties not exhibited by non-living and non-mental processes, and in many cases appear to violate the ubiquitous tendencies exhibited by these component interactions.

Wikipedia definition: Emergence: In philosophy, systems theory, science, and art, emergence is the way complex systems and patterns arise out of a multiplicity of relatively simple interactions. Emergence is central to the theories of integrative levels and of complex systems.

Chapter Summary

This chapter characterizes the term 'emegergence': its emergence in the 19th century, de-emergence in the late 20th century and more recent re-emergence.
Question: how does stuff learn new things without a teacher? Answer: stuff emerges.
Physics: emergence is relatively new phenomena in the universe.
Philosophy: although thought about by the Greeks, emergence brought to forefront by Darwin and natural selection.
History: British Emergentists (reductionists) - reduced to ashes by Jaegwon Kim [at Oxford University btw].
Philosophy: Davidson: No higher order event occurs without concomittant change at lower order.
Two types of emergence: 1. inorganic whole is sum of parts and 2. organic whole is greater than the sum of the parts.
Parodying the GE slogan: 'With general emergence: process is our most important product.'

5 A house of cards? - p164

Key words: Jaegwon Kim, Causal closure , Jerry Fodor, Supervenience, Donald Davidson, Part/whole, Mereology, Functionalism, Quantum physics, Quantum fields, Mark Bickhard,

This section: describes Kim's critiques of emergentist theories. Nevertheless some parts may have a meronic validity, but even these have issues. If it's organized all the way down, and nothing above can happen without without something below having happened, then how can anything emerge? So alternative ideas regarding emergence are needed - especially one that take time into consideration - a dynamic systems approach.
Topics covered: philosophy, quantum mechanics

Issue with emergence theories: possibility of double counting leading to infinite regression: An end result causing itself.

Jaegwon Kim challenges the popular assumptions of emergentists - forcing us to think outside the box.

Kim's critique based on a core issuee of emergence: supervenience

Donald Davidson defines:there cannot be two things that are physically identical but differ in some mental respects. Or an object cannot alter some mental respect without altering in some physical aspect.

Or there cannot be a change of thought without a change at the neuronal level.

If so, then every possible higher order property is dependent on a lower order property but then nothing can ever emerge.

No difference in the whole is possible without a difference in the parts. Therefore how could there be something in the whole that is not reducible to a combination of properties of the parts?

The part/whole study is critical and has pre-Socratic origins. See also mereology.

Kim uses mereology principle of defining parts and wholes so as to avoid double counting. p165

Functionalism, form and multiple realizibility. p166

Substance metaphysics.

Alternative needed.

Example: quantum physics: there no 'atoms'/ultimate small particle down there - just quantum fields.

Mereology only begins to succeed at a Newtonian level after statistical regularity of quantum fields have been taken into account. Remember this is just an example of failure, not itself leading towards a solution. p167

Mark Bickhard: to say that particles participate in organization, but are not themselves organized is pointless. Particle behavior is process-like and thus by definition organized. p187

If it's [turtles;-] organized all the way down, and nothing above can happen without without something below having happened, then how can anything emerge?

Another issue is synchronicity. Higher order properties were not always present. Life and mind are just about brand new in the universe. Emergence is emergent.

Looking at emergence as an ongoing dynamic system helps, so do the many new type of exemplars of emergence as well as new tools.

6 Complexity and 'chaos' - p169

Key words: Emergence, Cellular automata, John Conway, Game of Life, Suart Kaufman, Steven Wolfram, Phase space, Attractor, Edward Lorenz, Lorenz attractor, Complex_systems, Chaos theory, Synergetics, Intuition pump

This section: The development of computer algorithms in the late 20thC lead to emergence of genetics and AI This lead to re-emegence of emergence as a topic of interest..\
Topics covered: Computation, mathematics, physics

20th century: two reasons emergence reemerges: 1. organizations discovered within inorganic phenomena, 2. advent of computer simulations.

Unlikely the former would have recived the attention without development of the latter because computer controls enabled innumerable iterations upon the substrate enabling quasi-regularities to be identified.

Graphic representations of such processes enabled a perception of the process by humans.

These computer simulations became interesting in their own right - which led to the emergence of algorithms cellular automata.

Famous 1960s examples: John Conway's Game of Life demos emergence. See also work by Stuart Kaufman on connecting cells and Steven Wolfram on physical processes

The underlying algorithms produce non-linear results, but they are recursive

The solutions can be mapped out as a phase space - with trajectories of changing values. The solutions are not entirely regular but are not entirely chaotic either.

Trajectories tend to orbit particular zones, such a structure is termed an attractor.

Famous example: Edward Lorenz 1960s: Lorenz attractor an example of deterministic chaos

Spawns many types of investigation: complexity theory, chaos theory, synergetics, complex systems theory and others.

Two have become prominent: 1. genetic algorithms. 2. artificial intelligence.

These emergent processes are:

  • Non-mysterious
  • Mechanistic
  • Generate spontaneous regularities
  • Not dependent on anything extrinsic
  • Not a summation of intrinsic components
  • Exhibit properties that have no lower-level counterparts
  • Exhibit no end directed organization
  • Can be described as emergent
  • Provide a shift of perspective.
  • Uncluttered with historical baggage
  • Shifting the term 'emergence' from philosophical term to a more general usage
  • Handy as intuition pumps

Caveats: the meaning of the word 'emergence' continues to emerge and emergengce is presumed to have emerged in places it actually did not.
