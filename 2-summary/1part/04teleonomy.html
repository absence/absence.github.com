Chapter 4 ~ Teleonomy

Teleology is like a mistress to a biologist: he cannot live without her but he’s unwilling to be seen with her in public. — J B S Haldane

From the Incomplete Nature glossary:

Teleonomy (Teleonomic): Teleological in name only. A terminological distinction that would exemplify a middle ground between mere mechanism and purpose, behavior predictably oriented toward a particular target state even in systems where there was no explicit representation of that state or intention to achieve it
Teleological (Teleology): Purposive, or end-directed (the study of such relationships). Philosophically related to Aristotle’s concept of a “final cause”

From Wikipedia: Teleology:

is any philosophical account that holds that final causes exist in nature, meaning that design and purpose analogous to that found in human actions are inherent also in the rest of nature.

2 The Law of Effect - p109

This section:

  • Form and function obey natural selection.

  • As part of the process of natural selection, organisms adapt.
  • Law of effect

  • The mechanisms for producing both forms and functions are separate from the processes of preserving them

4 Blood, brains, and silicon - p124

This section:

  • Outlines areas of study where natural selection has been seen to replace teleology.

Key words: Humoral immune response, Hypermutation, Blanks slate [aka Tabula rasa], , B F Skinner, Karl Popper, Evolutionary epistemology, Donald T Campbell, Blind variation and selective retention, Gerald Edelman< Immunoglobulin [aka antibody], Artificial life

Topics covered: biology, philosophy

A success for Law of Effect: Humoral immune response.

Common sense suggested immune response was a blank slate - ready to accept what was presented to it.

Reality: in pre-natal state hypermutation produces vast array of forms. Later, when exposed to disease, with a bit of luck some of these forms bind with the enemy. If it fits, this is the signal to make more of the same.

Most will never be used. Fit is only approximate. No information is acquired.

B F Skinner and behaviorism: spontaneous exploratory behavior analogous to genetic variation. Reward or punishment analogous to selection pressures. Habit formation or extinction analogous to differential reproduction

Karl Popper's evolutionary epistemology; progressive adaptation of scientific knowledge to its empirical subject matter.

Donald T Campbell's blind variation and selective retention.

Gerald Edelman: start with an overabundance of neurons and connections. Keep the ones that best reflect converging signal correlations.

Artifical life: computer simulation of natural selection or unnatural selection. Teleogy free - though parameters are extrinsically determined. But remember it's only a game.

5 Fractions of life - p128

This section:

  • Considers replication and natural selection at the molecular level.
  • Dawkins provides a reasonable caricature of genes(software) and organisms(hardware) as an information processing system
  • But an orgamism is not a assembly of parts. An organism grows. An orgamism is not a machine, it's a different kettle of fish.
  • Thus Dawkin's and others' analyses - based on machine paradigms - are are either over-simplifications or incomplete or smuggle in patterns
  • - and thus create numerous homunculi.

Key words: DNA, RNA, Ernst Mayr, Richard Dawkins, Selfish Gene, Turing Machine, Jesper Hoffmeyer, Biosemiotics, Claus Emmeche, Prions, Paul Weiss, Élan vital

Topics covered: biology, philosophy

DNA and RNA mean that life may be understood in the terns of information processing. 'Algorithms' appears to be a reasonable caricature.

Even evolution may be explained in these terms with the introduction of errors into the code.

Richard Dawkins's Selfish Gene (1976): possible to think of evolution in informational terms - with genes as the organiational software

But DNA (software) needs an organism (hardware) to run in. For Dawkins; the organism is the medium through which genes influence their probability of being replicated

Jesper Hoffmeyer: selection external/mutations internal. But mutation is random

Claus Emmech: organism becomes passive meeting place for forces alien to itself.

Prions: good example of the complexity of self-replication

Dawkins is not incorrect, but equating information with pattern smuggles in systemic properties

Paul Weiss (1960s): Biology builds prosthetic devices to make up for amputations to the organic wholeness.

Organisms are not mere assembles of parts. They grow.

Élan vital hasn't been snuffed, it's just been pushed into smaller parts within parts - tiny simpler bits have to do the heavy lifting.
