Objective for the book

The objective for Incomplete Nature is to:

Resolve Descarte's mind-body problem based upon the scientific method.

This statement can be divided into three parts: the philosophical part, the scientific part and the resolution.

Wikipedia introduces its article on the mind-body problem as follows:

The mind–body problem in philosophy examines the relationship between mind and matter, and in particular the relationship between consciousness and the brain. The question, as Colin McGinn puts it, is how "technicolour phenomenology [can] arise from soggy grey matter.

Any resolution of the philosophical aspects must take into consideration the thinking of the major philosophical thinkers and tendencies.

Wikipedia introduces its article on the scientific method as follows:

The scientific method is a body of techniques for investigating phenomena, acquiring new knowledge, or correcting and integrating previous knowledge. To be termed scientific, a method of inquiry must be based on empirical and measurable evidence subject to specific principles of reasoning. The Oxford English Dictionary defines the scientific method as: "a method or procedure that has characterized natural science since the 17th century, consisting in systematic observation, measurement, and experiment, and the formulation, testing, and modification of hypotheses.

Any resolution of the scientific aspects of the problem must be able to take into account that thought you just had all the way down to whatever Higg's bosans may be in your brain or not in your brain as the case may be. In between, topics such as how do insticts and language arise, how did inorganic become organic, what was there before there was the second law of thermodynamics and more more must be addressed.

In other words, the resolution of the mind-body problem must take into account eveything we know.

Incomplete Nature is an attempt to bring together the concepts of what we do know from many sciences into a whole.

But wait, wait. There's more!

There's a lot we don't know. There's probably more out there that we don't know than that we do know.

So, as Deacon might say, the book is about (w)holes - because there are so many holes in our knowlesdge.

Book Parts

Deacon says the book is divided into three parts. The three parts are described as follows:

Part 1 ~ Chapters 1-5 ~ Outlining the Problem

"I show that the conceptual riddles posed by absential phenomena have not been dealt with, despite claims that they have been overcome, but rather that they have been swept under the rug in various ingenious ways. By critiquing the history of efforts to explain them or explain them away, I argue that our various efforts have only served to insinuate these difficulties more cryptically into our current scientific and humanistic paradigms."

Part 2 ~ Chapters 6-10 ~ Outlining an Alternative Theory

"I outline an alternative approach—a theory of emergent dynamics that shows how dynamical process can become organized around and with respect to possibilities not realized. This is intended to provide the scaffolding for a conceptual bridge from mechanistic relationships to end-directed, informational, and normative relationships such as are found in simple life forms."

Part 3 ~ Chapters 11-17 ~ Exploring the Implications

"Having laid the groundwork for an expanded science capable of encompassing these basic absential relations, I explore a few of the implications for reformulating theories of work, information, evolution, self, sentience, and value. This is a vast territory, and in these final chapters I only intend to hint at the ways that this figure/background shift in perspective necessarily reformulates how these fundamental concepts need to be reconsidered."
