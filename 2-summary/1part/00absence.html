Chapter 0 ~ Absence

In the history of culture, the discovery of zero will always stand out as one of the greatest single achievements of the human race. —Tobias Dantzig, 1930

Definition from Glossary
[page:Absential Absential]: The paradoxical intrinsic property of existing with respect to something missing, separate, and possibly nonexistent. Although this property is irrelevant when it comes to inanimate things, it is a defining property of life and mind; elsewhere (Deacon 2005) described as a constitutive absence

Chapter summary: Sets out the objective to solve the mind'mody problem using good science. Introduces the notion that what is not there matters and proposes the neologism 'ansenntial' to describe this phenomenom. Compares the discourse on this topic to the discovery and development of the concept of the number zero.

1 The missing cipher - p1

Key words: Theory of Everything, [page:Absential Absential],

This section: Deacon lays down his proposed rules of engagement with traditional philosophy and the scientific method. Absential is introduced as a neologism, defined and parsed. Just as the pixels your see in front of you have no meaning in themselves, what is not there matters and may well have phyisical consequence. A good theory for this must be based on good science.

The current scientific Theory of Everything implies that we don’t exist, except as collections of atoms.

Consider the sentences you are now reading. The meaning is not in the squiggles, not in the sounds made, not even in the neurons being fired in your brain

These sentences lacks the properties that normally cause things to happen. Information has no mass, momentum, charge, no solidity, no presence of any sense. Moreover these sentences could even be nonsense and have no correspondence to reality. Nevertheless if that nonsense caused you to wonder, it could still influence the way you act.

Despite the surreal aspects, These sentences were written to convey a meaning. And you are expending effort in order to receive this information.

Thus, there's something not-there there. And what is not there may have physical consequences.

What is absent matters. Longings, desires, passion, appetite, mourning, aspiration are all ideas about things not present or that don't exist and have no physical attributes - but these are the stuff of life.

A shovel is not just a shovel. And it's not a hole in the ground. Yet it is a method of creating holes more easily.

A defining property of life and mind, a causal role for absence seems to be absent from the natural sciences.

Definition: the [page:Absential Absential] are phenomena whose existence are determined with respect to an essential non-existence.

A complete theory of the world must make sense of what is not there and how that affects what is there.

2 What matters? - p3

Key words; Charles Darwin, Dynamical system, Gregory Bateson, Heinz von Foerster, Humberto Maturana, Francisco Varela, Autopoiesis, René Descartes, [page:Consciousness Consciousness], Materialism, David Chalmers, 'Me'

This section: 19th century begins to see the world materialistically. Late 20th century sees failure to complete on consciousness. Are we left with Descartes? Is an observer is still required? Incomplete Nature will argue that consciousness - non-material phenomena, absent from the physical world - may have causal, physical results.
Disciplines included: philosophy, biology, mathematics, cognitive science

Most of science focuses on the things that are actual and present.

Inorganic things tend to fall into disorder. When confronted by things that become 'organized' - a non-actual, non-physical trait - people have historically tended to ascribe them to a 'designer'.

In 19th C: Darwin postulates organization without a designer: natural selection. People begin to observe numerous self-organizing inorganic processes. Scholars could believe the entire gamut of organic and mental process could be described mechanistically in non-absential terms. The book is built upon such work. There are, however, caveats...

Example: dynamical systems [an area of mathematics used to describe the behavior of complex dynamical systems, usually by employing differential equations or difference equations.]: limited because whatever causes an energetic difference must be seen to exist.

Beginning 1980s, Bateson, von Foerster, Maturana, Varela et al start to see that dynamical systems could not be universal.

Maturana and Varela (1980): develop concept of autopoesis - self-creating dynamics. But this takes too much for granted. Requires an observer. Does not account for the absential.

Thus Descartes's mind-body problem continues. Something is still missing.

Materialism [the only thing that exists is matter or energy] cannot hold true here.

David Chalmers: the emergence of experience goes beyond what can be derived from physics.

Consciousness is what is left after you take away all the physical bits.

Tenet of the book: Consciousness may not be there, but it can still be materially, causally efficacious.

Not only does consciousness remains unaccounted for. So does 'me'.

3 Calculating with absence - p8

Key words: zero, Zeno's paradox

This section: How the absential might be like zero - ignored, then distrusted, now opening new vistas.
Disciplines covered: math, philosophy

Not the first time this happens. It was the same thing with zero.

Discovery/Invention enabled many other new things.

Zero and absential have many of the same qualities - some good and some maddening.

Dealing with the absential - as with dividing by zero - has caused fear among scientists, but could lead to many new discoveries.

4 A Zeno’s paradox of the mind - p11

Key words: Zeno's paradox

This section: a restating of the previous section.
Disciplines covered: philosophy

Ignoring the absential causes the invention of tricks, hacks and workarounds.

In science, essential features of live considered illusory - opens door for religious thinking.

Inability to integrate the absential handicaps us - as the absence of zero used to.

Hello! We do need a scientific proof/explanation of our own reality.

Envisage the physical and the meaningful coming together.

5 “As simple as possible, but not too simple” - p13

Key words: Albert Einstein, 'As simple as possible, but not too simple'

This section: characterizes the work, outlines the goals of the writing style, describes the organization of the book and exhorts the reader to suspend disbelieef.

Incomplete Nature is a first step towards unifying [mind and body].

This work: Will be scorned, ridiculed. Will take effort and time. Will be a risk. But avoidance causes cynical, negative thinking.

Will be difficult, not obvious as answers are not where they were meant to be. Yet: 'Can do!'

Incomplete Nature to be:

  • "As simple as possible, but not too simple"
  • Designed for curious, well-educated reader
  • Minimum prior knowledge and technical paraphernalia [equations and formulas]
  • Neologisms will be coined

Book is in three sections:

  1. Chapters 1-5: Show that science has not dealt with the absential.
  2. Chapters 3-10: Outlines an alternative approach - that allows for the absential
  3. Chapters 11-17: Explores the implications of a science that encompasses the absential

Incomplete Nature is a first baby step. Kindly play along and you will glimpse the future of science.
